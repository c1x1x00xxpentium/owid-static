Afforestation

by Hannah Ritchie

Which countries are gaining, and which are losing forest?

Before we look at trends in afforestation across the world specifically, it’s useful to understand the net change in forest cover. The net change in forest cover measures any gains in forest cover – either through natural forest expansion or afforestation through tree-planting – minus deforestation.

This map shows the net change in forest cover across the world. Countries with a positive change (shown in green) are regrowing forest faster than they’re losing it. Countries with a negative change (shown in red) are losing more than they’re able to restore.

A note on UN FAO forestry data

Data on net forest change, afforestation and deforestation is sourced from the UN Food and Agriculture Organization’s Forest Resources Assessment. Since year-to-year changes in forest cover can be volatile, the UN FAO provide this annual data averaged over five-year periods.

Afforestation

One way that forest cover can increase is through natural forest expansion – when trees return to land that was previously forested but had been converted to an alternative land use. The other is through afforestation – deliberate restoration through seeding and tree-planting.

Do rich countries export deforestation to other countries?

There is a marked divide in the state of the world’s forests. Most rich countries, across Europe, North America and East Asia are regrowing their forests, whilst many low-to-middle income countries are losing them. 

This is not particularly surprising: most countries move through the ‘forest transition model’ where they cut down forests for cropland and fuel, until they reach a transition point where they begin to restore them. Today’s rich countries have all been through this journey in the past. We look at why countries follow this path and specific country examples in a related article.

95% of global deforestation now takes place in the tropics. Rich countries might argue that it’s not their responsibility: they are ‘doing their bit’ by regrowing forests at home. But this argument doesn’t account for the impact countries might have abroad, through the products that they import. After we account for international trade, does it still hold true?
This discussion is similar to the debate about ‘offshoring CO2 emissions’: many people argue that rich countries have only been able to reduce their domestic emissions because they’ve ‘offshored’ the production of goods elsewhere. This is rarely the case – as we discuss here, it’s true that most rich countries have higher emissions when we account for the goods they import and export (consumption-based CO2 emissions) but they’ve still fallen regardless of what metric we use.

Which countries are net importers or exporters of deforestation?

Does this apply to forests? Are countries ‘offshoring’ deforestation elsewhere? To investigate this question, researchers Florence Pendrill et al. (2019) quantified the deforestation embedded in traded goods between countries.1 They did this by calculating the amount of deforestation associated with specific food and forestry products, and combining it with a trade model to trace where these products move through international supply chains to the final consumer.

In the map we see the net deforestation embedded in trade for each country. This is calculated by taking each country’s imported deforestation and subtracting its exported deforestation. This means that countries with a negative value (in brown) are net exporters, and countries with positive values (in green) are net importers. For example, after we adjust for all the goods that Brazil imports and exports, it experienced more deforestation at home than it created elsewhere. The UK, in contrast, created more deforestation in other countries than it did domestically.

Although there is some year-to-year variability [you can explore the data use the timeline on the bottom of the chart from 2005 to 2013] we see a reasonably consistent divide: most countries across Europe and North America are net importers of deforestation [i.e. they’re driving deforestation elsewhere] whilst it’s many subtropical countries that are cutting down trees to meet this demand.

It is therefore true that many rich countries are ‘offshoring’ deforestation to poorer ones.

Are countries importing more deforestation than they’re regrowing domestically?

Many rich countries are driving deforestation in other parts of the world. But are they creating more deforestation elsewhere than they are regenerating in forests at home? This would give us some sense of whether they’re still having a net positive impact on global forests.

If, for example, Country A was responsible for the deforestation of 25,000 hectares in other countries but was restoring its own forests at a rate of 50,000 hectares per year, it might argue that it was still having a positive impact on global forests. There are some issues with such an argument, such as the fact that tropical forests are often more productive (they store more carbon) and are richer sites of biodiversity, hence we shouldn’t treat one hectare of tropical and temperate forest equally. But, for now, we’re going to simplify and treat any hectare of forest equally.

In the chart we see the comparison between the change in domestic forest area, and deforestation driven by imported goods.2 On the y-axis we have the domestic change in forest area: this is shown only for countries which have a positive change i.e. their forest area is increasing. Since there is often year-to-year variability in deforestation or reforestation rates, this is shown as the five-year average. On the x-axis we have imported deforestation. The grey line marks where domestic regrowth of forests is exactly equal to imported deforestation. Countries that lie along this line would have a net-neutral impact on global forests: they could argue that deforestation they’re creating through imported products is offset by the regrowth of forests at home.

Countries which lie above the grey line – such as the United States, Finland, China – restore more forest each year domestically than they import from elsewhere. For example, the US ‘imported’ 64,000 hectares of deforested land, but increased its domestic forest area by 275,000 hectares. More than four times as much. On balance, they add to the global forest stock. Countries below the line – such as the UK, Germany and Norway – are not growing forests fast enough to offset the deforestation they’re creating elsewhere. The UK ‘imported’ 34,000 hectares of deforestation but increased its domestic forests by only 19,000 hectares. These countries might boast record levels of afforestation at home, but they’re still having a net negative impact on the world’s forests. There is a strong argument there that they are offshoring deforestation.

Imported deforestation provides an important caveat to the forest transition model

The ‘Forest Transition Model’ says that countries tend to follow a U-shaped curve of forest change as they develop: first they lose forests, then eventually regain them. Long-term forestry data from a range of countries supports this.

But, this model of change only considers changes in domestic forests. And as we’ve seen above, this is only part of the story. The fact that some countries are creating more deforestation elsewhere at a faster rate than they’re regrowing their own forests provides an important caveat to this Forest Transition Model.

In fact, Pendrill et al. (2019) found that 79% of exported deforestation ended up in ‘post-transition’ countries: those that had stopped losing domestic forests. In other words, poor countries export deforestation and rich countries import most of it. As we saw earlier, not all rich countries import more deforestation than they regrow. But, some do and at the very least, some of this reforestation is offset. In total, for all post-transition countries, one-third of the net reforestation gains were offset by imports of embodied deforestation. This does not completely undermine the Forest Transition Model, but does suggest that at least part of it has been enabled by international trade and offshoring of deforestation.3

The solution to this is not a simple one. Many might suggest that rich countries should stop importing foods from countries at-risk of deforestation. But there are other aspects to consider: international trade is important for socioeconomic development. Many farmers rely on international buyers to earn a living and improve their livelihoods. The solution to this problem is therefore not as simple as cutting out trade. It’s about adopting stricter guidelines on what suppliers to source from, and implementing zero-deforestation policies that stop the trade of goods that have been produced on deforested land.

There are multiple ways in which countries can help end deforestation – both at home and elsewhere in the world. This is something we explore in much more detail in a follow-up article. Until rich countries take them seriously we should keep their domestic reforestation targets in perspective with their net impact on global forests. Sometimes these restoration programmes pale in comparison to the deforestation they’re driving elsewhere.
