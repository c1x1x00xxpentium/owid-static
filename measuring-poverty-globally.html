Measuring poverty globally

by Joe Hasell
January 11, 2022

Summary

This article was prepared for a presentation Joe Hasell made to students at the Department of Social Policy and Intervention, University of Oxford.

  • A global approach to measuring poverty faces a number of challenges: vast inequality, data quality.
  • These is exacerbated by different strands of poverty research itself inhabiting different ‘worlds’: with very different concepts, metrics, data sources and methods applied in different contexts and disciplines.
  • Connecting up these ‘worlds’ of poverty research gives us Robust insights into the global poverty trends are possible, however. They involve:
    • Multiple poverty lines, as well as other complementary metrics
    • ‘Triangulating’ across different data sources, and across monetary and non-monetary dimensions of welfare

Global economic inequality: what matters most for your living conditions is not who you are, but where you are

Who is considered ‘poor’ varies enormously across richer and poorer countries

Finding a global approach to measuring poverty is also complicated by the very different standards of living that are considered to be ‘poor’ across richer and poorer countries.

The world’s richest countries tend to adopt poverty lines equivalent to around $30 a day.

The world’s poorest countries tend to adopt poverty lines more than 15 times lower – around $1.90 per day. This is known as the International Poverty Line, and has been adopted by the World Bank and the UN in order to monitor progress against extreme poverty.

Global economic inequality: what matters most for your living conditions is not who you are, but where you are

Connecting the different ‘worlds’ of poverty research

Whilst the differences between the richest and the poorest people in the world today are very large, it’s a mistake to see countries as dividing cleanly into poor vs rich, developed vs developing, or even – as some still describe things – as living in distinct ‘First’ and ‘Third’ worlds.

This view made some sense in the mid-20th century – after some countries, but not others, had achieved substantial growth compared to pre-industrial times.

But it has been clear for a long time that that view misrepresents how living standards now vary across the world. (See for instance the excellent TED talk by Hans Rosling from 2006 on this point).

The majority of the world live not at the ends of the distribution, but in the middle: somewhere between the poverty lines set in the world’s poorest and richest countries.

Global Economic Inequality

Whilst it no longer makes sense to think about the distribution of living standards in terms of ‘developed’ and ‘developing’ worlds, different strands of poverty research can often have this aspect of seeming to occupy different worlds.

Different concepts, metrics, data sources and methods tend to get applied in different contexts and disciplines.

Poverty in richer countries is often – though not universally – measured using a relative .

Based on household income survey data, typically where incomes are ‘equivalized’ – adjusted for the size and composition, to account for ‘economies of scale’ in the household.

It might be studied in Social Policy, in dialogue to the modern welfare state.

The chart here shows a commonly-used metric for analysing and discussing poverty in rich countries: the share of population with an equivalized disposable household income below 60% of the median. This metric, for instance, is the official ‘at risk of poverty’ definition adopted by the EU.
