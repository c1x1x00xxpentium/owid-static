Measuring poverty globally

by Joe Hasell
January 11, 2022

Summary

This article was prepared for a presentation Joe Hasell made to students at the Department of Social Policy and Intervention, University of Oxford.

  • A key challenge to measuring global poverty is finding an approach that is relevant and informative across richer and poorer countries.
  • This is exacerbated by different strands of poverty research itself inhabiting different ‘worlds’: with very different concepts, metrics, data sources and methods applied in different contexts and disciplines.
  • Connecting up these ‘worlds’ of poverty research – by using multiple poverty lines and other complementary measures, and by ‘triangulating’ across across different data sources and dimensions of welfare – gives us robust insights into the global poverty trends.
  • What this shows us is that, whilst there has been a substantial global decline in the most extreme poverty, by the standards of today’s rich countries the history of global poverty reduction is really only just beginning.

Global poverty in an unequal world: Who is considered poor in a rich country? And what does this mean for our understanding of global poverty?

Yet some countries are much poorer than others

A big challenge for understanding poverty globally, however, is finding an approach to measuring poverty that is relevant and informative across richer and poorer countries.

The large inequality we see across countries makes a poverty line of $30 a day unhelpful for understanding poverty globally and how it is changing.

In countries as diverse as Malawi, India, Mexico and China almost everyone – more than 90% – would all likewise be considered ‘poor’ using such a poverty line.

Connecting different ‘worlds’ of poverty research and data

A global understanding of poverty is also made more difficult by the different concepts, metrics, data sources and methods applied in different contexts and disciplines.

It can often feel like different aspects of poverty research occupy separate ‘worlds’.

How poverty is typically measured in poorer countries

Poverty in poorer countries is typically measured using household survey data – most commonly surveys of households’ consumption.

For international comparisons this data is usually expressed in per capita terms – where each individual is counted as having a consumption level equal to their household’s total, divided by the number of people (children plus adults) in the household.

Poverty is then measured against an ‘absolute’ line that is fixed in terms purchasing power across time and place.

The chart here shows a typical set of data: the share living on less than $1.90 per day, which is the definition of extreme poverty adopted by the UN and World Bank.

Extreme poverty: how far have we come, how far do we still have to go?

How poverty is typically measured in richer countries

Until only very recently, such efforts by the World Bank to measure poverty around the world didn’t include rich countries.2

And in general the approach to measuring poverty in rich countries is somewhat different.

Firstly, poverty estimates are normally based on surveys of households’ income – typically expressed in ‘equivalized’ terms, where an adjustment is made for the size and composition of the household to account for economies of scale in consumption.

Secondly, poverty in most rich countries tends to be measured in relative terms: against a poverty line that rises with average incomes.

The chart here shows a typical set of data: the share of population with an equivalized disposable household income below 60% of the median. This metric, for instance, is the headline definition used by the EU.

How economic growth is typically measured

There is also a big gap between the way economic growth is typically measured, as compared to poverty.

Rather than household surveys, macroeconomists and others wanting to understand economic growth typically rely on measures from the National Accounts, such as GDP per capita – as shown in the chart here.

One important benefit of national accounts data is that it is generally available over much longer timeframes. And, economic historians have devised methods to extend the approach much further into the past still.

Economic Growth

We can’t understand global poverty without understanding economic growth

Why is a understanding of economic growth important? After all, it refers only to the aggregate gains in income, not to how those gains are distributed.

Economic growth – or its lack – does not solely determine the extent of poverty. Countries with high inequality, such South Africa and Brazil, have much higher poverty rates than those countries at a similar average level of income which have lower inequality.

But it does place hard bounds on what is possible. A country in which the average income is close to the poverty line is a country in which the vast majority will be in poverty, no matter how incomes are distributed.

The chart here shows the share of population in living on less than $5.50 per day – a higher poverty line used by World Bank – plotted against average incomes.

We see that the relationship is very close.

How much economic growth is necessary to reduce global poverty substantially?

Recent trends in economic growth are part of the reason we should adopt a more global approach to measuring poverty

Economic growth – and the national accounts data used to measure it – is fundamental to understanding how we arrived at the global distribution of income we see today

This data shows us that, in pre-industrial times almost everyone lived in extreme poverty. Historian Michail Moatsos estimates that around three-quarters of the world population in 1820 “could not afford a tiny space to live, some minimum heating capacity, and food that would not induce malnutrition.”3

Following industrialisation some countries grew their economies and left the very worst poverty behind, but many – often those subjected to extractive colonial administrations – did not.

More recently, fast economic growth in many formerly poor countries means that such a binary distinction between rich and poor, ‘developed’ and ‘developing’, no longer makes sense.

The majority of the world today live not at the ends of the distribution, but in the middle: somewhere between the poverty lines set in the world’s poorest and richest countries.

We need a global approach to measuring poverty that reflects that reality.

Global Economic Inequality

A global approach to measuring poverty means connecting up these different worlds of poverty research

This is the same chart of national poverty lines and average incomes as before, now with both measures shown on a logarithmic scale.

What I like about this chart is that shows that the worlds of research and data I just introduced are not as far apart as they can seem.

Although high-income countries tend to set their poverty lines and measure poverty differently to how low and middle-income countries do, there is no real ‘break’ here.

National poverty lines tend to rise with average income: either in an automatic way through a relative poverty line, or through periodic upward revisions of an absolute line.

It is economic growth – rising average incomes – that allows a country not only to reduce poverty, but to ratchet-up the level of living standards by which poverty is judged.

To understand global poverty trends you need multiple poverty lines

Multiple poverty lines are needed in order to have an approach that is informative and relevant around the world.

  • informative: a poverty line in which almost all or almost no one is counted as poor irrespective of major shifts in income is not useful for measuring progress or motivating action against poverty.
  • relevant: a global approach needs to engage with wide range of living standards that are considered ‘poor’ across countries with higher and lower average incomes.

Extreme poverty is falling: How is poverty changing for higher poverty lines?

Complementary monetary metrics

There are other summary indicators – other than the ‘headcount ratios’ measured against absolute poverty lines shown above – that capture important dimensions of poverty.

The poverty gap – capturing the depth of poverty

A weakness of the ‘headcount ratio’ – the share of population falling below the poverty line – is that it is not sensitive to how far household incomes fall short of the poverty line. It measures the prevalence but not the depth of poverty.

The poverty gap index is one measure that tries to address this. In the World Bank’s usage it is defined as the mean shortfall of the total population from the poverty line (counting the non-poor as having zero shortfall), expressed as a percentage of the poverty line.4

Other indexes that are sensitive to the depth of poverty include the squared poverty gap index and the Watts index – see Ravallion (2016) for a summary.5

A downside to such measures though is that they are less straightforward to understand and interpret.

Relative poverty – within individual countries

The focus on relative measures of poverty in richer countries is based on an established current in political and ethical thought that emphasises the importance of societal functionings like participation or the absence of shame.

But such concerns of course apply not only in very rich countries.

Extreme poverty is falling: How is poverty changing for higher poverty lines?

Relative poverty – across the world

A relatively recent idea is to track global relative poverty – by summing up across countries the number of people falling below a relative poverty line set in relation to their own country’s average income.

This idea is now implemented by the World Bank in its ‘Societal Poverty Line’ (another recommendation in Monitoring Global Poverty), and is shown in the chart here.

Such an approach can be thought as a natural extension of the relative approach long used as standard in many rich countries.

However, when used to aggregate poverty over countries with very different average living standards it hides important differences. The SPL counts equal someone living on $2 a day in a very poor country and someone living on $30 in a very rich country.6

The Societal Poverty Line and national poverty lines vs median income or consumption – World Bank (2018)7

Triangulating across data sources

A key strategy often emphasised by Tony Atkinson was to ‘triangulate’ across data sources to see where they disagree and where they help reinforce one other.

Household survey data vs national accounts: different, but not so different

For a more detailed discussion of the use of survey vs national accounts data in global poverty measurement see Roser and Hasell (2021).8

The two data key data sources from which we can track incomes – household survey data and national accounts series, such as GDP per capita – in broad terms give us a similar picture about how living standards compare across countries and over time. The two measures are very closely correlated.

However, there are important conceptual differences in what they try to measure and particular comparisons over time or across countries can look different depending on which set of data we consider.

For example, the means observed in household surveys are generally smaller than GDP per capita, and the gap is generally greater for middle- and high- income countries.

A particularly stark example of divergence is in India, in which GDP per capita over the last generation rose around twice as fast as the average level of consumption indicated in household surveys.

How sensitive are global poverty estimates to the choice of data source?

Because GDP per capita is generally higher than the averages found in survey data, and the gap widens as incomes rise, global poverty estimates are lower and follow a steeper downward trend when countries’ incomes are anchored to GDP per capita rather than survey means.

You can see that in the table here from Pinkovski and Sala-i-Martin (2016) in which this comparison is made.9

They show that trends in GDP per capita track the amount of nighttime light visible in satellite imagery more closely than household survey data does.

This, they argue, is evidence that GDP per capita gives the more accurate view of how living standards have changed and, hence, of global poverty trends.

However, which data source provides the best basis for assessing poverty, or how they are to be best combined, is still a debated topic amongst researchers. (See Atkinson 2019, pp. 139–43).10

An example of ‘triangulation’

Part of the apparent discrepancy in poverty trends as evidenced by the two data sources owes to the fact that a given dollar-value poverty line picks up a different part of the global distribution in each case.

In 1980 the lower quartile, median and upper quartile of the global distribution were around 2-3 times larger when anchoring incomes to GDP per capita as opposed to household survey means.11

Since incomes subsequently shifted unevenly across the global distribution, it is unsurprising that poverty trends look different whether calculated in terms of national accounts- or survey-anchored incomes.

If instead we set the poverty lines at specific percentiles of the respective distributions, the evolution of global poverty looks quite similar across both data sources.

Although discrepancies between national accounts and survey data point to important uncertainties in terms of the level of poverty, they in fact reinforce one another when it comes to the key trends.

Non-monetary measures of poverty and wellbeing

Clearly income is not the only thing that matters to people.

And whilst monetary and non-monetary dimensions of poverty are generally closely correlated, there is substantial variance.

Monitoring non-monetary measures is essential therefore, both to broaden our conception of poverty, but also as a sense-check on trends in monetary poverty in the context of significant uncertainty and data limitations.

This can be done at the aggregate level.

For instance, we see in this chart that countries with higher poverty rates tend to have higher child mortality. No country with an extreme poverty rate above 5% has achieved a child mortality rate lower than 1%.

But there is also huge variability: Pakistan and Mozambique have comparable rates of child mortality even though the latest data from 2019 indicates that their extreme poverty rates were 4% and 62% respectively.

Since different indicators of well-being can move in different directions across time and space, researchers and policymakers often construct indexes that aggregate across various dimensions of deprivation.

The Human Development Index is one such index made at the aggregate national level which averages income, health and education indicators.

Again, as we see in the chart, it is closely, but imperfectly, correlated with GDP per capita.

Non-monetary dimensions of wellbeing can also be assessed and aggregated at the household level.

The Multidimensional Poverty Index – published by the Oxford Poverty & Human Development Initiative (OPHI) – is constructed from ten weighted indicators across three core dimensions: health, education and living standards.12

A person is counted as multi-dimensionally poor if they are deprived in a third or more of the ten (weighted) indicators.

Conclusion: Two centuries of progress and still a very long way to go

A focus on extreme poverty shows us that there has been substantial progress in living standards globally relative to pre-industrial times.

Such a focus remains essential for motivating and coordinating action to improve living conditions in the world of the roughly 700 million people now living in such extreme deprivation.13

But a global understanding of poverty – of where we’ve come from, where we stand today, and what we should aim for in the future – requires a broader approach that connects the perspectives of richer and poorer countries today.

From such a perspective, the history of global poverty reduction has really only just begun.
