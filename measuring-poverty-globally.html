Measuring poverty globally

by Joe Hasell
January 11, 2022

Summary

This article was prepared for a presentation Joe Hasell made to students at the Department of Social Policy and Intervention, University of Oxford.

  • A global approach to measuring poverty faces a number of challenges: vast inequality, data quality.
  • These is exacerbated by different strands of poverty research itself inhabiting different ‘worlds’: with very different concepts, metrics, data sources and methods applied in different contexts and disciplines.
  • Connecting up these ‘worlds’ of poverty research gives us Robust insights into the global poverty trends are possible, however. They involve:
    • Multiple poverty lines, as well as other complementary metrics
    • ‘Triangulating’ across different data sources, and across monetary and non-monetary dimensions of welfare

Global poverty in an unequal world: Who is considered poor in a rich country? And what does this mean for our understanding of global poverty?

Yet some countries are much poorer than others

A big challenge for understanding poverty globally, however, is finding an approach to measuring poverty that is relevant and informative across richer and poorer countries.

The large inequality we see across countries makes a poverty line of $30 a day unhelpful for understanding poverty globally and how it is changing.

In countries as diverse as Malawi, India, Mexico and China almost everyone – more than 90% – would all likewise be considered ‘poor’ using such a poverty line.

Challenge: Connecting different ‘worlds’ of poverty research and data

A global understanding of poverty is also made more difficult by the different concepts, metrics, data sources and methods applied in different contexts and disciplines.

It can often feel like different aspects of poverty research occupy separate ‘worlds’.

How poverty is typically measured in poorer countries

Poverty in poorer countries is typically measured using household survey data – most commonly surveys of households’ consumption.

For international comparisons this data is usually expressed in per capita terms – where each individual is counted as having a consumption level equal to their household’s total, divided by the number of people (children plus adults) in the household.

Poverty is then measured against an ‘absolute’ line that is fixed in terms purchasing power across time and place.

The chart here shows a typical set of data: the share living on less than $1.90 per day, which is the definition of extreme poverty adopted by the UN and World Bank.

Extreme poverty: how far have we come, how far do we still have to go?

How poverty is typically measured in richer countries

Until only very recently, such efforts by the World Bank to measure poverty around the world didn’t even try to include rich countries at all.

Data on poverty in rich countries is more typically based on surveys of households’ income.

Typically these incomes are expressed in ‘equivalized’ terms – where they are adjusted for the size and composition of the household, to account for economies of scale in consumption.

And poverty tends to be measured in relative terms: against a poverty line that shifts with average incomes.

The chart here shows a typical set of data: the share of population with an equivalized disposable household income below 60% of the median. This metric, for instance, is the official ‘at risk of poverty’ definition adopted by the EU.

How economic growth is typically measured

There is also a big gap between the way economic growth is typically measured, as compared to poverty.

Rather than household surveys, macroeconomists and others wanting to understand economic growth typically rely on measures from the National Accounts, such as GDP per capita – as shown in the chart here.

One important benefit of national accounts data is that it is generally available over much longer timeframes. And, economic historians have devised methods to extend the approach much further into the past still.

Household survey data vs national accounts: Different, but not so different

There are many differences between household survey and national accounts data, including important conceptual differences in what they try to measure.

Both have limitations in terms of data quality. And which source provides the most accurate picture depends on the question being asked.

But because these two measures are often used in different contexts – for measuring poverty, or measuring economic growth – it’s easy to forget that both sets of data aim to capture the same thing: how standards of living vary over time and place.

Not surprisingly then, they are highly correlated.

We can’t understand global poverty without understanding economic growth

Of course economic growth refers only to the aggregate gains in income, not to how those gains are distributed.

Countries with high inequality – such South Africa and Brazil – have higher poverty rates than those countries at a similar average level of income which have lower inequality.

Nevertheless the correlation between poverty and average incomes is very strong.

A country in which the average income is close to the poverty line is a country in which the vast majority will be in poverty, no matter how incomes are distributed.

Recent trends in economic growth are part of the reason we should adopt a more global approach to measuring poverty

Economic growth – and the national accounts data used to measure it – is fundamental to understanding how we arrived at the global distribution of income we see today

This data shows us that, in pre-industrial times almost everyone lived in extreme poverty.

Following industrialisation some countries grew their economies and left the very worst poverty behind, but many – often those subjected to extractive colonial administrations – did not.

More recently, fast economic growth in many formerly poor countries means that such a binary distinction between rich and poor, ‘developed’ and ‘developing’, no longer makes sense.

The majority of the world today live not at the ends of the distribution, but in the middle: somewhere between the poverty lines set in the world’s poorest and richest countries.

We need a global approach to measuring poverty that reflects that reality.

Global Economic Inequality

A global approach to measuring poverty means connecting up these different worlds of poverty research

This is the same chart of national poverty lines and average incomes as before, now with both measures shown on a logarithmic scale.

What I like about this chart is that shows that the worlds of research and data I just introduced are not as far apart as they can seem.

Although high-income countries tend to set their poverty lines and measure poverty differently to how low and middle-income countries do, there is no real ‘break’ here.

National poverty lines tend to rise with average income: either in an automatic way through a relative poverty line, or through periodic upward revisions of an absolute line.

It is economic growth – rising average incomes – that allows a country not only to reduce poverty, but to ratchet-up the level of living standards by which poverty is judged.

To understand global poverty trends you need multiple poverty lines

Multiple poverty lines are needed in order to have an approach that is informative and relative around the world.

  • informative: a poverty line in which almost all or almost no one is counted as poor irrespective of major shifts in income is not useful for measuring progress or motivating action against poverty.
  • relevant: a global approach needs to engage with wide range of living standards that are considered ‘poor’ across countries with higher and lower average incomes.
