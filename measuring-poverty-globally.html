Measuring poverty globally

by Joe Hasell
January 11, 2022

Summary

This article was prepared for a presentation Joe Hasell made to students at the Department of Social Policy and Intervention, University of Oxford.

  • A global approach to measuring poverty faces a number of challenges: vast inequality, data quality.
  • These can sometimes be exacerbated by the use of different concepts, data, methods across poverty research from different disciplines or with different geographic or topic focuses.
  • Robust insights into the global poverty trends are possible, however. They involve:
    • Multiple poverty lines, as well as other complementary metrics
    • ‘Triangulating’ across different data sources, and across monetary and non-monetary measures

The challenge: finding a global approach to poverty measurement in very unequal world

No country has solved the problem of poverty

The chart here shows the share of the population living below $30 a day in different countries.

$30 a day is a level of income that is broadly reflective of the value of poverty lines set in rich countries.

Even in Norway – one of the richest and most equal countries in the world – a significant share of the population live in poverty.

Yet some countries are much poorer than others

The world is characterised by vast inequalities. The living standards whose standards of living are worlds apart.

That poses a challenge to measuring poverty globally.

So in order to measure poverty globally, we need an approach that distinguish the enormous differences in living standards that exist between rich and poor countries, whilst being alive to the existence of poverty even in the richest countries.

Different ‘worlds’ of poverty research

As well countries’ standards of living, it can also feel like different strands of poverty research occupy totally different worlds: with different concepts, metrics, data sources and methods applied in different disciplines.

Poverty in rich countries is often measured. Based on household income survey data, typically where incomes are ‘equivalised’ – adjusted for the size and composition, to account for ‘economies of scale’ in the household.

It might be studied in Social Policy, in dialogue to the modern welfare state.

Typical : LIS share below 60% median, which is the ‘at risk of poverty’ definition used by the EU for instance.

Poverty in poorer countries is often measured . Household survey data, but more commonly of household consumption rather than income. Household incomes are generally expressed in per capita terms – simply divided by the number ()

Typical: share below $1.90 per day. International poverty line adopted by the UN and World Bank.

Economic growth across rich and poor countries alike is often measured using National Accounts data, for instance GDP per capita.

Economic growth across a whole country studied by macroeconomists.

Measuring global poverty means connecting these ‘worlds’ up

Practical hinderance: hard to compare.

But deeper. Sometimes that you can lose sight of the fact that these two measures – (monetary) poverty and economic growth – all aim at discussing the same thing: the distribution of welfare.
